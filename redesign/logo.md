# KFZ Digital — Brand Identity & Logo Brief

> **Working language**: English. German is the live market but all working docs stay in English.

---

## 1. Strategic Foundation (logo is downstream of this)

### 1.1 Positioning Statement
**For** German car owners who dread the registration office,
**KFZ Digital is** the first end-to-end digital registration platform
**that** turns a half-day bureaucratic ordeal into a 15-minute task,
**unlike** existing services that only sell plates or service single steps.

### 1.2 Brand Archetype
**The Magician × The Sage**
- *Magician*: makes friction disappear (15 minutes vs. 4 hours)
- *Sage*: knows the rules, holds legal authority (i-Kfz certified, KBA partner)

**Not**: The Hero (we are not "fighting bureaucracy" — we are bypassing it calmly).
**Not**: The Everyman (we are premium, not folksy).

### 1.3 Brand Voice
- **Calm**, not energetic
- **Confident**, not loud
- **Precise**, not casual
- **Modern**, not trendy
- **Quietly German** — engineered, exact, unembellished (think Braun, Leica, Porsche Design — not Berlin start-up)

### 1.4 Single Owned Word
If the brand owns one word: **"Precision"** (Präzision).
Every visual decision must answer: *does this feel precise?*

## 3. Committed Creative Direction

> Two directions only. Not five. Pick one in round one; the other becomes the backup.

### Direction A (PRIMARY) — "The Plate"

**Concept**: The logo *is* a precision-engineered German license plate, abstracted into a monogram.

**Why this wins**:
- "KFZ Digital" (Kfz = Kraftfahrzeug, vehicle) — the mark *embodies the category*.
- Instantly recognisable as German (EU blue strip + D code on real plates).
- Owns the category visually — competitors won't follow because it feels too obvious until you see it executed well.
- Scales from favicon (just the plate silhouette) to billboard (full lockup with EU strip).
- Works in monochrome — survives newspaper print, dark mode, embroidery.

**Design DNA**:
- Horizontal pill, **2.5:1 ratio** (real EU plates are 4.7:1 — we tighten for visual weight)
- **Rounded corner radius**: subtle, 8% of height — *not* fully pill-shaped; we want the plate to be readable as a plate
- Left vertical band (where the EU/D would sit) becomes a **solid colour accent strip** — this is our signature design element
- Inside the plate: lowercase wordmark **kennzeichen** in a custom geometric sans
- Optional: subtle inset shadow on the plate face to hint at machined aluminium (use very sparingly — never in flat applications)

**Symbol-only variant** (favicon, app icon, partner badge):
- Just the plate silhouette with the coloured accent strip and a single character: either `K` or the EU stars motif
- Square-ratio version: stack the plate inside a rounded square

**Why competitors haven't done this well**:
- Most try a literal plate (skeuomorphic, dated, too much detail)
- The trick is *abstracting* the plate — keeping the proportions, accent strip, and corner radius, but treating it as a wordmark frame, not a representational object

### Direction B (BACKUP) — "The Imprint"

**Concept**: A monogrammed **K** embossed into a precision aluminium-feeling card.

**When this wins instead**: If "The Plate" is rejected as too on-the-nose, or if partners need a more flexible, less product-specific mark for white-label co-branding.

**Design DNA**:
- Square or rounded-square plaque, 1:1 ratio
- A single geometric **K** — uppercase, custom-drawn, with one *signature cut* (a single negative-space slice that hints at speed/motion without using arrows)
- Two-tone: deep slate plaque + warm white or single accent on the K
- Reads as an engraved trade-stamp, not a typographic exercise

## 4. Typography

### Wordmark
**Recommended font families** (designer picks one, customises 2–3 glyphs):
- **PP Neue Montreal** — clean, slightly humanist, German-feeling geometric
- **Söhne Breit** — Klim Type Foundry, neutral and precise (used by Stripe, NYT)
- **Geist Sans** — Vercel's font, modern geometric, free
- **Inter Display** — *banned*. Generic, overused. Do not use.

**Custom touches required**:
- Modify the "K" to have a sharper terminal — becomes the logo's signature glyph
- Adjust the "k/z" pairing for optical balance (the most awkward letter pair in the wordmark)
- Tighten tracking to roughly **-2%** for the wordmark; default tracking for body usage

### Supporting typography
- **Headlines**: same family, display weight
- **Body**: same family, regular weight at 16px / 1.55 line height
- **Numerals**: tabular figures only (we display prices, durations — tabular = trustworthy)

## 5. Colour System

### The decision rule
**One primary anchor + one signature accent + neutrals. That is it.**
No gradients in the logo. No three-colour combinations. Restraint.

### Recommended palette

| Role | Token | Hex | Use |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Anchor** | `ink` | `#0E1116` | Wordmark, body type, plate face in light mode |
| **Signature accent** | `signal` | `#1E5BFF` | The plate's accent strip. Sole emotional colour. |
| **Surface** | `paper` | `#F6F5F2` | Off-white with warmth — never pure `#FFFFFF` |
| **Surface dark** | `obsidian` | `#0A0B0E` | Dark-mode background, never pure black |
| **Hairline** | `hairline` | `rgba(14,17,22,0.08)` | Borders, dividers — never solid grey |

### Colour reasoning (not Wikipedia)
- **Why blue, specifically `#1E5BFF`**: it's the colour of the EU strip on real German plates — visual continuity with the product. But shifted slightly cooler and more saturated than the real plate blue so it owns a *brand* position, not a *factual* one.
- **Why warm off-white surface**: cooler whites read clinical/medical. Warm off-white reads premium, considered, hand-finished.
- **Why `#0E1116` not `#000000`**: pure black has no depth on screen. Slightly desaturated ink reads as ink, not as a colour-absence.

### What we explicitly reject
- Green (used by every "trust" brand — done to death)
- Gradients (instantly dates the logo)
- Two-blue palettes (corporate-SaaS cliché)
- Orange accent (zulex.de territory)
- Yellow (looks like a Pinterest infographic)

## 6. Anti-Patterns (hard NOs)

If any deliverable contains these, reject and re-brief:

- ❌ A car silhouette of any kind
- ❌ A location pin
- ❌ A checkmark (any colour, any orientation)
- ❌ A lightning bolt or speed-line motif
- ❌ A globe / network-of-dots / "connectivity" cliché
- ❌ A steering wheel
- ❌ A gradient inside the logo
- ❌ An emoji-style flat illustration
- ❌ A 3D render of anything
- ❌ Skeuomorphic chrome, brushed metal, or glossy reflections
- ❌ An ornamental serif headline ("hand-crafted" / artisan styling)
- ❌ All-caps wordmark in a thick geometric sans (Uber, Airbnb cliché)
- ❌ A hexagon or honeycomb
- ❌ A shield, seal, or crest (avoid bureaucratic visual codes — we are *bypassing* bureaucracy)
- ❌ Inter, Roboto, Helvetica, Arial, or Open Sans

## 9. Critique Framework (for evaluating concepts)

Score each concept 1–5 against:

1. **Precision** — does it feel exact, engineered, considered?
2. **Ownership** — could a competitor swap their name in and use the same mark?
3. **Restraint** — is anything in here that doesn't earn its place?
4. **Scale** — does it survive 16px and 1200px equally?
5. **Monochrome** — does it work without colour?
6. **Time-test** — will this look modern in 2030, or is it 2024-trendy?

A concept must score ≥4 on all six to advance.

**Do not use**: "modern, professional, trustworthy, dynamic, innovative" — these adjectives have no visual instruction. The prompts above describe *materials*, *proportions*, *constraints* — which is what AI generators actually respond to.

## 11. Open Questions to Resolve Before Designer Brief-Out

1. Is the company name on the final live product going to be **"KFZ Digital"** or **"kfzdigital.de"**? The wordmark differs significantly.
2. Will partners (Zulassungsstellen, dealerships, white-label clients) carry our mark on their materials? If yes, the symbol-only variant becomes the primary deliverable.
3. Do we have budget for a custom-drawn wordmark (~€8k–15k from a typographer like Klim, Pangram Pangram, GrilliType), or are we using an off-the-shelf premium font?
4. Is Denis open to **a single primary direction in round 1**, or does he need to see multiple? (Single direction → far better final result; multi-direction → safer but produces compromised final.)
